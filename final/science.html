The Science of Tornadoes

How Tornadoes Form

Tornadoes form when warm, moist air collides with cold, dry air, creating instability in the atmosphere. This often occurs in thunderstorms, where wind shear (a change in wind speed or direction with height) causes the air to rotate, forming a mesocyclone. If conditions are right, a funnel cloud descends from the storm, becoming a tornado upon touching the ground.

Conditions for Tornadoes

  • Warm, moist air near the ground, often from the Gulf of Mexico.
  • Cold, dry air aloft, typically from the jet stream.
  • Wind shear, which creates rotation within the thunderstorm.
  • Atmospheric instability, leading to strong updrafts.

Types of Tornadoes

  • Rope Tornado: Thin, rope-like appearance, often in the dissipating stage.
  • Wedge Tornado: Wide, wedge-shaped, often associated with the most destructive storms.
  • Multi-Vortex Tornado: Contains multiple smaller vortices within the main funnel.
  • Waterspout: A tornado over water, typically weaker than land tornadoes.
